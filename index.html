Novel
Bloom
Delilah S. Dawson (2023)
~190 pages Debut novel: No Avg rating: 3.8★
Theme fit
5 / 5
CW Heavy

Vibe: A sapphic, cottagecore‑goes‑fatale nightmare that feels like Yellowjackets meets The Lighthouse in a queer art‑house horror short film.

Rosemary “Ro” Dutton, a newly tenured literature professor, falls into a whirlwind romance with Ash, a charismatic artisan whose perfect cottagecore life hides dangerous secrets. As Ro’s life becomes increasingly entangled with Ash’s, she begins to suspect that love and obsession are indistinguishable—and that something ancient and hungry might be using their relationship to bloom.

Content warnings: Psychological horror, gaslighting, obsession, manipulation, implied supernatural violence, toxic relationship dynamics, body‑centric horror.

Novel
Brother
Ania Ahlborn (2015)
~330 pages Debut novel: No Avg rating: 3.8★
Theme fit
4 / 5
CW Extreme

Vibe: A relentlessly grim, rural‑slasher‑meets‑family‑drama that plays like The Texas Chainsaw Massacre filtered through Sharp Objects.

In rural West Virginia, nineteen‑year‑old Michael lives with a family who abduct, torture, and murder young women while presenting as ordinary to the outside world. Groomed into complicity and terrified of breaking away, he’s torn between loyalty to his monstrous kin and a fragile, almost impossible longing for a different life, making the story a study in learned violence and the pull of familial obsession.

Content warnings: Extreme graphic violence, serial murder, torture, sexual violence, rape, necrophilia‑adjacent imagery, kidnapping, child abuse, incest‑coded dynamics, strong body horror, animal harm, pervasive psychological abuse.

Novel
Misery
Stephen King (1987)
~330 pages Debut novel: No Avg rating: 4.2★
Theme fit
5 / 5
CW Heavy

Vibe: A bruising, chamber‑piece psychological thriller that hits like Room crossed with Fatal Attraction, set in a snow‑locked Colorado cabin.

After a car crash in a blizzard, bestselling novelist Paul Sheldon is “rescued” by Annie Wilkes, his self‑proclaimed number‑one fan who keeps him bedridden, medicated, and under surveillance. When she learns he has killed off her favorite character, her devotion curdles into violence and she forces him to rewrite the story, turning the novel into a claustrophobic battle over authorship, bodily autonomy, and the horror of obsessive fandom.

Content warnings: Kidnapping, prolonged captivity, physical torture, graphic injuries (including mutilation), implied sexual tension, psychological abuse, threat of sexual violence, isolation, substance withdrawal.

Theme fit
3 / 5

Vibe: A lurid, underground‑monster fable that feels like Tales from the Crypt directing Underworld in a neon‑dyed horror‑punk club.

Aaron Boone, a troubled man plagued by visions of monstrous beings called the Night Breed, is manipulated by his psychiatrist into believing he’s a serial killer. As he’s hunted by both the police and the figures from his nightmares, Boone discovers a hidden city of supernatural outcasts and is forced to decide where he truly belongs, turning the story into a dark‑fantasy blend of identity crisis, persecution, and monstrous self‑acceptance.

Content warnings: Body horror, graphic violence, mutilation, demonic and cult imagery, implied cannibalism, sexualized horror, gore, psychological manipulation, depictions of mental‑illness stigma.

Short work
Things Have Gotten Worse Since We Last Spoke
Eric LaRocca (2021)
~120 pages Debut book: No Avg rating: 3.9★
Theme fit
5 / 5
CW Extreme

Vibe: A stomach‑churning, epistolary online‑relationship horror that feels like Catfish colliding with The Guilty in a very dark, early‑2000s LGBT chat‑room hellscape.

The story unfolds through emails and instant messages between two lonely young women, Agnes and Zoe, who meet on an LGBT forum in 2000 and spiral into an increasingly extreme, S&M‑driven relationship they never intended. Presented entirely through their digital correspondence, the novella tracks how obsession, despair, and violent fantasies bleed into their real lives, blurring the line between consent and compulsion as each pushes the other further.

Content warnings: Graphic sexual violence, extreme sadomasochism, self‑harm, emotional and psychological abuse, homophobia, animal death, gore, financial exploitation, online grooming, disturbing kink and bodily degradation.

Novella
The Monster of Elendhaven
Jennifer Giesbrecht (2019)
~160 pages Debut book: Yes (first book‑length) Avg rating: 4.1★
Theme fit
4 / 5
CW Extreme

Vibe: A grim, queer‑noir fairy tale that plays like Penny Dreadful co‑written with Lovecraft in a poisoned, fog‑choked coastal town.

Johann, a seemingly immortal creature dredged up from the polluted harbor of Elendhaven, is drawn into the schemes of Florian Leikenbloom, a frail but ruthless sorcerer bent on revenge against the prosperous South that abandoned his family. As Johann becomes Florian’s monster‑for‑hire, the two form a toxic, obsessive partnership that turns the rotting city into a stage for murder, plague magic, and class resentment.

Content warnings: Explicit body horror, gore, cannibalism, sexual violence, queer‑coded predatory intimacy, murder, plague and corpse imagery, child death, toxic relationship dynamics, gaslighting, psychological abuse.
